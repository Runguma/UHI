    Urban Heat Profile

    April, 2024 Un-Habitat

    In the Gaza Strip, Palestine, the assessment of urban heat islands before the war revealed an intriguing scenario. The mean value of 44.57 contrasted with a negative Urban Heat Island (UHI) Intensity of -1.03, indicating that the urban areas were relatively cooler than the surrounding rural regions.

    This daytime interpretation is brought about by the concept of urban heat sinks. Unlike traditional urban heat islands where cities trap and retain heat, the built-up areas in Gaza Strip appeared to act as heat sinks, absorbing and dissipating heat more effectively than the surrounding sand and barren land.

    This phenomenon was likely influenced by the difference in heat absorption between urban areas and the arid, sandy environments. The urban fabric, comprising various materials like concrete and asphalt, has a lower heat capacity and thermal conductivity compared to the surrounding sand and barren land. As a result, the urban areas heated up more slowly during the day, creating a localized cooling effect.

    The unique heat dynamics observed before the war were poised to play a crucial role in determining the effects of the conflict on the urban fabric. The destruction of buildings and infrastructure would have altered the heat absorption and dissipation patterns, potentially leading to a reversal of the urban heat sink effect. Additionally, the debris and rubble from damaged structures could have increased the heat retention capacity of the urban areas, exacerbating the impact of the conflict on the local climate and living conditions.
